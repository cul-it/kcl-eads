Biographical / Historical

The Lawrence Textile Strike, also known as the Bread and Roses strike, took place between the months of January and March 1912 and was led by the Industrial Workers of the World (IWW). In January 1st of that year, a Massachusetts law went into effect cutting female mill workers work week from 56 to 54 hours, effectively giving them a two-hour pay cut. Shortly after, workers learned that in addition to their cut in hours, they would also receive a cut in wages.
The strikers included immigrants from over 50 countries and a large number of women, the primary workforce in the textile industry. Police were brought in to quell the strikers and in January 1912, a striker Anna LoPizzo was killed during a protest - both sides blaming the other for her death. Two IWW strike leaders, Joseph Ettor and Arturo Giovannitti were arrested on charges of being accessories to murder, dispite being at a different location at the time. After this, IWW organizers Bill Haywood and Elizabeth Gurley Flynn came to Lawrence to head up the strikers.
After the death of Anna LoPizzo, martial law was declared but the violence between the police and the strikers continued. This led to the tactic of sending hundreds of strikers' children to families in New York, New Jersey, and Vermont in order to avoid the violence â€“ a move that drew national attention and sympathy for the striking workers. As the strike went on, congressional hearings would publicly reveal the unsafe working conditions in Lawrence mills and lead to calls to investigate the powerful companies such as the American Woolen Company that controlled much of the textile manufacturing in New England.
By March, mill owners agreed to a 20 percent raise throughout New England. The win was short lived however, as the IWW quickly lost its influence to other unions and many of the mill owners would eventually move operations to the Southern United States where there were fewer unionized shops.
